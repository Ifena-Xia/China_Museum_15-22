1. Background

China has been actively protecting and promoting its intangible cultural heritage since 2005. Several scholars suggest that this intangible heritage "boom" was triggered by the successful registration of the Dano festival onto the UNESCO intangible heritage list by South Korea in the same year (Wu, 2020; Ke, 2022). China's heritage projects are believed to enhance its cultural soft power in the global arena while contributing to domestic nation-building efforts (Sen, 2010). Beyond UNESCO registration, China has invested heavily in museum construction. Museum count, size, and spending all jumped in 2007, and subsidies rose with them; scholars were calling it a boom by 2018 (Zhang, 2021).

The boom has not slowed. By the end of 2024 China had 7,046 registered museums, roughly one per 200,000 people, more than nine in ten free to enter, and they recorded about 1.49 billion visits that year (National Cultural Heritage Administration, 2024). The question this study asks is not whether the sector has grown, but whether the money has spread evenly.

As a crucial platform for the manifestation of cultural objects and public education (Blunden, 2016), museums constitute part of a politics of identity. Despite their legal designation in Chinese legislation as "a basis for patriotic education" (Bollo & Zhang, 2017), museum expansion has received far less scholarly attention than heritage tourism more broadly.

The intuitive expectation is that wealthier provinces invest more. Zhang and Courty (2021) found the opposite for 1996 to 2015: museum growth was relatively balanced across regions, which they read as a deliberate push for accessibility and cultural equity. This study asks whether that balance held after 2016.

Museum development in China has always been intertwined with political and economic forces. After the founding of the People's Republic of China, museum development became part of the Chinese Communist Party's governing policy, serving economic, ideological, and political needs (Lu, 2014). Although absolute regulation relaxed after the 1980s reforms, museums remain instruments of cultural policy, and local officials have at times treated museum construction as a visible marker of performance (Shan, 2009).

Through examining the National Museum of China in Beijing and the Liaoning Provincial Museum in Shenyang, Gideon Shelach-Lavi argues that modern Chinese public museums embody an inclusive yet standardized narrative of Chinese history, one that establishes continuity between the past and the present (Shelach-Lavi, 2019). This resonates with broader observations about cultural nationalism: with the upsurge of nationalism and skepticism toward the Party since the 1990s, state intervention in cultural projects has intensified (Walton, 2012; Zhao, 2004). Patriotic themes, introduced partly as a replacement for Marxist ideology, have been linked to Confucian tradition to legitimize the Party's authority over cultural interpretation (Lin, 2023; Mufson, 1996).

2. Data and Method

Data come from the Statistical Yearbook of Cultural Relics of China (SYCRC) and statistical reports published by the Ministry of Culture and Tourism (MCT). The Yearbook provides provincial museum counts, while MCT reports supply information on tourist reception, expenditure, subsidy, and related metrics. This study focuses on three variables: GDP per capita, total museum subsidy, and total museum expenditure, disaggregated by Eastern, Central, and Western regions.

Subsidy refers to direct financial support from the government to museums, ensuring operational sustainability and tied to policy changes. Zhang and Courty demonstrate that rising museum attendance in China is largely explained by policies stimulating infrastructure development and reducing admission fees, which means demand is driven more by government investment than by public cultural demand per se (Zhang & Courty, 2022).

Expenditure represents total operational spending, excluding infrastructure costs: staff salaries, exhibitions, educational activities, and professional training. Following Frey and Meier (2006), expenditure serves as a proxy for museum quality and overall service investment.

Zhang and Courty used per capita museum subsidy and expenditure against per capita GDP. However, given the considerable disparity in population distribution (the 2020 census shows Eastern provinces account for nearly 40% of the national population, up 2.5% from 2010), absolute subsidies in Eastern provinces would far exceed those in Central and Western ones, disproportionate to the actual number and scale of museums present. A large museum in a densely populated area may receive more absolute funding than multiple smaller museums in sparsely populated regions, even if per capita figures suggest equal investment.

Guangdong holds more than five times Inner Mongolia's population and so reports a lower subsidy per head, yet it puts more than twice as much money into each square meter of museum. Per capita figures, in other words, can flip the ranking that funding intensity actually shows.

This study therefore uses subsidy and expenditure per square meter of museum floor space as the primary investment metrics. This approach controls for differences in museum size and distribution, offering a clearer picture of funding intensity and equity.

Which test backs which claim. The correlations reported below are Pearson coefficients pooled across all 217 province-years, on the per-square-meter metrics. Two choices matter, because the result moves with them. First, per square meter rather than total: total spending tracks wealth mechanically, since richer provinces simply have more museum floor space. Second, pooled rather than year by year: with only 31 provinces in a single year, even a real association of this size fails to reach significance, so the pooled estimate is the one the claims rest on. 3. Regional Patterns

MCT reports divide China into three regions. The Eastern region includes Beijing, Tianjin, Liaoning, Shanghai, Jiangsu, Zhejiang, Fujian, Shandong, and Guangdong. The Central region includes Hebei, Shanxi, Jilin, Heilongjiang, Anhui, Jiangxi, Henan, Hubei, Hunan, and Hainan. The Western region includes Inner Mongolia, Guangxi, Chongqing, Sichuan, Guizhou, Yunnan, Tibet, Shaanxi, Gansu, Qinghai, Ningxia, and Xinjiang.

Three series, read side by side, set up the whole question. The East stays far ahead on wealth across the study period. Yet its lead all but vanishes once the eye moves to subsidy per square meter, and only partly returns for operating expenditure. The wealth ranking and the funding ranking are not the same ranking.

4. Correlation and the Key Finding

Pooling all 217 province-years and correlating each funding measure with GDP per capita turns that visual impression into numbers. The result is a gradient, not a single verdict.

Subsidy intensity per square meter shows no significant association with provincial wealth. Operating expenditure per square meter is weakly aligned. Museum size is strongly aligned. Plotting the weakest and the strongest of these side by side states the thesis in one view.

Money for buildings, not for running them.

The picture is a gradient. Subsidy intensity per square meter is not aligned with provincial wealth, operating expenditure is weakly aligned, and museum size is strongly aligned. In plain terms, provinces get money for buildings, not for running them.

A flat subsidy gradient need not mean any province chose to decouple funding from wealth. Central fiscal transfers to poorer western and minority regions would flatten the same gradient with no province deciding anything, and museum size, which does track wealth, sits behind the absolute figures as a confounder. The defensible claim is the association; the mechanism stays a candidate until a budget source pins it down.

Read against Zhang and Courty (2021), this both confirms and complicates them. Their equity-oriented reading holds for operating support, where the regions sit close together. It does not hold for the physical stock, since wealthier provinces still build larger museums, so the balance is in running costs rather than in bricks. The lesson for measurement is that per capita yardsticks, the usual tool, can mistake a big building for a well-funded one.
